3:T17e4,<p>Ask ten engineers about cover letters and you&#39;ll get ten answers, most of them dismissive. The honest position is somewhere in the middle: a generic cover letter is worthless and occasionally harmful, while a short, specific one is one of the cheapest advantages available in a competitive process. The difference is entirely in whether it could have been sent to any other company.</p>
<h2>When it&#39;s worth writing one</h2>
<p>Write one when:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The application form has a message field.</strong> Leaving it blank next to candidates who filled it in is a needless handicap.</li>
<li><strong>You&#39;re changing direction</strong> — different stack, different domain, career switch, returning after a gap. Your CV raises a question; the letter answers it before anyone has to guess.</li>
<li><strong>You genuinely know the product</strong> or have used it. This is the strongest possible opening and almost nobody has it.</li>
<li><strong>It&#39;s a small company or a startup.</strong> Founders read these. At small companies, the letter is frequently the deciding factor between two similar CVs.</li>
<li><strong>The post asks for one,</strong> obviously — including as a filter for people who follow instructions.</li>
</ul>
<h2>When it doesn&#39;t matter</h2>
<p>Skip it, or keep it to two lines, when:</p>
<ul>
<li>You&#39;re applying through a large company&#39;s automated system where it&#39;s optional and likely unread.</li>
<li>A recruiter is submitting you and a conversation has already happened.</li>
<li>You have a referral. The referral does the work a cover letter attempts.</li>
<li>The role is an exact match for your CV and there&#39;s nothing to explain.</li>
</ul>
<p>The cost of a generic one isn&#39;t zero: reviewers notice a letter clearly written for a different company, and it undermines the CV attached to it.</p>
<h2>What hiring managers actually want to know</h2>
<p>Whoever reads it is trying to answer three questions quickly:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Why this job?</strong> Not &quot;why a job&quot; — why this one, at this company.</li>
<li><strong>What&#39;s the evidence you can do it?</strong> One concrete example beats every adjective.</li>
<li><strong>Is there anything my process would misread?</strong> Gaps, switches, an unusual background.</li>
</ol>
<p>Everything else is filler.</p>
<h2>A structure that works</h2>
<p>Keep it under 200 words. Four short paragraphs.</p>
<p><strong>Paragraph one — why this company, specifically.</strong> Name something real. What the product does, a technical decision they wrote about, a problem you know is hard from their space. &quot;I&#39;ve used [product] for two years and the [specific feature] is what got me interested in how you handle [technical problem]&quot; is worth more than any statement of enthusiasm.</p>
<p><strong>Paragraph two — your single most relevant piece of evidence.</strong> One project, with a result. What you built, the constraint you worked under, what happened. Numbers where they&#39;re honest. This is the paragraph that earns the interview.</p>
<p><strong>Paragraph three — the thing your CV doesn&#39;t explain.</strong> The stack switch, the gap, the reason a backend engineer is applying for a platform role. One or two sentences, stated plainly, no apology. If nothing needs explaining, delete this paragraph.</p>
<p><strong>Paragraph four — a clean close.</strong> What you&#39;d like to happen next, and a link to something they can look at.</p>
<h2>An example, in outline</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>I&#39;ve been using [product] since [when] — the [feature] is the reason my team stopped building our own [thing]. I saw you&#39;re working on [problem], which is close to what I spent the last two years on.</p>
<p>At [company] I owned [system] through [constraint — a migration, a growth phase, a rewrite]. [What changed: latency, cost, reliability, delivery time.] The hardest part was [genuine difficulty], and I&#39;d be happy to walk through how we approached it.</p>
<p>You&#39;ll notice I&#39;ve mostly worked in [X] and this role is [Y]. I moved deliberately: [one-sentence reason], and I&#39;ve been building [evidence] in that direction.</p>
<p>Happy to talk any time. Here&#39;s [something they can look at].</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That&#39;s it. Nobody wants a page.</p>
<h2>Mistakes that cost you interviews</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Opening with &quot;I am writing to apply for the position of…&quot;</strong> Every reviewer has seen this ten thousand times. Use the first line to say something only you could say.</li>
<li><strong>Restating your CV in prose.</strong> They already have it, attached, right there.</li>
<li><strong>Describing yourself with adjectives.</strong> &quot;Passionate, detail-oriented team player&quot; is unfalsifiable and therefore ignored. Show the trait through an example or leave it out.</li>
<li><strong>Length.</strong> A full page is not thoroughness; it&#39;s a signal you can&#39;t prioritize.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting to change the company name.</strong> It happens constantly and it ends the application.</li>
<li><strong>Explaining a gap defensively.</strong> One straightforward sentence closes the topic. Three anxious paragraphs open it.</li>
<li><strong>Letting a language model write the whole thing.</strong> Reviewers now recognize the register instantly, and the generic result is precisely the failure mode you&#39;re trying to avoid. Use it to tighten your own draft, not to generate one.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A cover letter is worth writing exactly when you have something specific to say: you know the company, you have one strong piece of evidence, or your CV raises a question worth answering first. Keep it under 200 words, lead with something only you could have written, and attach something they can look at. Skip it entirely rather than send a generic one — a template letter costs you more than no letter at all.</p>
