3:Tdd3,<p>Posting a job and waiting is only half of sourcing. The strongest developers are frequently <em>passive</em> — happily employed and not scrolling job boards — which means the best candidates may never see your listing unless you go find them. Here&#39;s a practical guide to where technical talent actually is and how to reach them.</p>
<h2>Active vs. passive candidates</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Active candidates</strong> are looking now. Reach them with a great listing on the right board.</li>
<li><strong>Passive candidates</strong> aren&#39;t looking but would move for the right opportunity. Reach them with targeted, personal outreach.</li>
</ul>
<p>A healthy pipeline uses both. Relying only on inbound applications limits you to whoever happens to be searching this week.</p>
<h2>Post where developers gather</h2>
<p>For inbound, fit beats reach. A developer-focused job board puts your role in front of an audience that&#39;s already technical, so your applications are more relevant and your screening load is lower. Pair that with job alerts — many strong candidates set up alerts and respond fast when a matching role appears.</p>
<h2>Sources for passive candidates</h2>
<p>When you want to go find people, the richest sources are where developers do their work and share it:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Open-source communities.</strong> People whose contributions you admire are, by definition, people who can do the work.</li>
<li><strong>Technical writing and talks.</strong> Bloggers and speakers signal depth and communication skill.</li>
<li><strong>Professional networks.</strong> Your own team&#39;s networks are gold — referrals from engineers you trust convert well.</li>
<li><strong>Communities of practice.</strong> Niche forums and groups around specific stacks or domains.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Write outreach that gets replies</h2>
<p>Developers get a flood of generic recruiter spam and ignore almost all of it. To stand out:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Be specific.</strong> Reference their actual work — a project, a talk, a contribution. Show you did your homework.</li>
<li><strong>Be honest about the role.</strong> What&#39;s the problem, the stack, the level, the comp range?</li>
<li><strong>Be brief and human.</strong> A short, genuine note beats a templated wall of buzzwords.</li>
<li><strong>Respect their time.</strong> Make it easy to say &quot;not now&quot; and to reconnect later.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Build a referral habit</h2>
<p>Your engineers know other good engineers. Make referrals easy and part of the culture — not a once-a-year campaign. Referred candidates tend to be higher-fit and faster to close. Just be careful that referrals don&#39;t narrow your team&#39;s diversity; pair them with broader sourcing.</p>
<h2>Nurture a talent pipeline</h2>
<p>Not everyone is available the moment you reach out. Keep a warm list of people you&#39;ve connected with and check back when the timing fits. Sourcing is a long game; the candidate who says &quot;not now&quot; may say &quot;yes&quot; in six months.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Great sourcing combines inbound (a strong listing on a developer-focused board) with outbound (specific, respectful outreach to passive candidates you find through their work). Do both, write like a human, and nurture relationships over time — and you&#39;ll never be dependent on whoever happens to be searching today.</p>
