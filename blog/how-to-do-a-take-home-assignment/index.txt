3:T1ee5,<p>Take-home assignments are where a lot of strong candidates quietly lose offers — not because they can&#39;t code, but because they misjudge what&#39;s being evaluated. They over-build, they under-document, or they spend twelve hours on something that was meant to take three and still get rejected.</p>
<p>The reviewer is not looking for the most feature-complete submission. They&#39;re looking for evidence you&#39;d be good to work with on a codebase they maintain.</p>
<h2>Before you start: decide whether to do it</h2>
<p>Not every take-home deserves your evenings. Reasonable ones state a time limit, describe what&#39;s being evaluated, arrive after you&#39;ve spoken to a human, and don&#39;t resemble work they&#39;d otherwise pay for.</p>
<p>Push back, politely, when:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>There&#39;s no time limit</strong> — ask for one. &quot;Roughly how long should this take?&quot; is a fair question and the answer is informative.</li>
<li><strong>It&#39;s clearly production work</strong> for their actual product. That&#39;s free labour and it&#39;s worth declining.</li>
<li><strong>It&#39;s the first step</strong>, before any conversation. Asking for hours before you know anything about the role is a bad trade — ask for a call first.</li>
<li><strong>It would take more than about four hours unpaid.</strong> Asking &quot;is there a shorter version, or could we pair instead?&quot; is entirely reasonable and often accepted.</li>
</ul>
<p>Many companies offer a live pairing session as an alternative if you ask. If you interview better in conversation than in isolation, ask.</p>
<p>Declining a genuinely unreasonable assignment rarely costs you a job worth having.</p>
<h2>Scope it before you write anything</h2>
<p>The most expensive mistake is starting to code immediately.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Read the brief twice</strong> and list the explicit requirements. Build exactly those.</li>
<li><strong>Note what they said they&#39;re evaluating.</strong> If the brief says &quot;we care about how you structure code and handle errors,&quot; then structure and error handling get your time, and styling gets none.</li>
<li><strong>Ask clarifying questions.</strong> This is not cheating — it&#39;s the behaviour they want at work. A short, specific email (&quot;should the API handle concurrent updates to the same record, or is that out of scope?&quot;) is itself a positive signal.</li>
<li><strong>Set a hard time budget</strong> and decide in advance what you&#39;ll cut. Deciding under time pressure at 11pm produces worse choices.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Build the boring things well</h2>
<p>Reviewers form their impression from craft, not cleverness:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Make it run first time.</strong> A clear setup command, tested on a clean machine. Nothing sours a review faster than a submission that won&#39;t start.</li>
<li><strong>Structure it sensibly.</strong> Recognisable layout, obvious file names, no everything-in-one-file and no fifteen layers of abstraction for a small exercise.</li>
<li><strong>Name things well.</strong> The cheapest quality signal available.</li>
<li><strong>Handle errors and edge cases.</strong> Empty input, missing fields, failing network calls. This is where most submissions are thin and where you can easily stand out.</li>
<li><strong>Write some tests.</strong> Not exhaustive coverage — tests for the things that actually matter, which demonstrates judgment about risk.</li>
<li><strong>Commit in logical steps</strong> with clear messages. Reviewers often read the history, and one giant commit tells them nothing about how you work.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Do not over-build</h2>
<p>Gold-plating is the most common failure, and it reads as poor judgment rather than enthusiasm. If the brief asks for a small API, do not add authentication, a Docker Compose stack, a frontend, a caching layer, and CI.</p>
<p>You&#39;ll be assessed on whether you did what was asked, at good quality, within the stated time. Someone who adds twenty hours of unrequested infrastructure has demonstrated they can&#39;t scope work — which is a real concern in a colleague.</p>
<p>The correct outlet for extra ideas is the README.</p>
<h2>The README is where you win</h2>
<p>Most candidates submit code with a two-line README. This is the single biggest opportunity in the whole exercise, because it&#39;s where you get to explain your thinking — which is what they&#39;re actually trying to evaluate.</p>
<p>Include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>How to run it.</strong> Exact commands, top of the file.</li>
<li><strong>What you built</strong> and which requirements it covers.</li>
<li><strong>Your key decisions and why</strong> — this library over that one, this structure, this data model. Two or three sentences each.</li>
<li><strong>Assumptions you made</strong> where the brief was ambiguous.</li>
<li><strong>What you deliberately left out</strong>, and why. &quot;I skipped auth because the brief scoped it out&quot; shows you read carefully.</li>
<li><strong>What you&#39;d do with more time.</strong> Name the real weaknesses. Reviewers respect a candidate who identifies the same gaps they spotted; it demonstrates you have the standards even when the clock didn&#39;t allow them.</li>
<li><strong>How long it took</strong>, honestly.</li>
</ul>
<p>That section turns a submission from something they must evaluate into something you&#39;ve already explained. It&#39;s also indistinguishable from good professional practice, which is the point.</p>
<h2>Using AI tools</h2>
<p>Assume you&#39;re allowed unless told otherwise, and assume you&#39;ll be asked to explain everything you submit.</p>
<p>The practical rule: <strong>never submit a line you couldn&#39;t defend or modify on a call.</strong> Most companies now debrief take-homes live, and the failure mode they&#39;re screening for is exactly the candidate who can&#39;t explain their own architecture. Use the tools the way you would at work — then read, understand, and take ownership of the result.</p>
<p>If the brief asks you not to use them, respect that and say what you did.</p>
<h2>Prepare for the debrief</h2>
<p>Most processes follow the submission with a conversation. Prepare for it as seriously as the code:</p>
<ul>
<li>Be able to <strong>justify every significant decision</strong>.</li>
<li>Know <strong>where it&#39;s weak</strong> and say so before they ask.</li>
<li>Be ready to <strong>change a requirement live</strong> — &quot;how would you handle a hundred times the data?&quot; is a near-certain question.</li>
<li>Be ready to <strong>extend it</strong> in a pairing session.</li>
</ul>
<p>Candidates who are honest about limitations consistently outperform those who defend everything.</p>
<h2>Small things that matter</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Submit on time</strong>, or say early if you need longer. Silence past a deadline is its own signal.</li>
<li><strong>Don&#39;t publish it publicly</strong> if the company asked you not to — and consider not doing so anyway, since future candidates will find it.</li>
<li><strong>Include the time you spent</strong> and don&#39;t inflate or deflate it.</li>
<li><strong>Ask for feedback</strong> if you&#39;re rejected. Many companies give it, and it&#39;s the most useful information you&#39;ll get all search.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A take-home tests judgment more than skill. Scope it to exactly what was asked, build the unglamorous parts well, resist adding anything unrequested, and put your real thinking in the README — decisions, assumptions, known weaknesses. Be able to defend every line in the debrief. And treat an unreasonable assignment as information about the company, not an obstacle you&#39;re obliged to clear.</p>
