3:T19e6,<p>Frontend roles are the easiest to describe badly. Post a list of framework names and you&#39;ll get a pile of applications from people who have used those frameworks and can&#39;t build an interface anyone wants to use. The candidates worth hiring are filtering for something else: who owns design decisions, how much say they&#39;ll have, and whether the codebase is a pleasure or a punishment.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s a template that answers those questions, followed by the parts specific to hiring frontend engineers.</p>
<h2>The template</h2>
<h3>Frontend Developer at [Company]</h3>
<p><strong>Location:</strong> [City / Remote — [region] / Hybrid]
<strong>Compensation:</strong> [$X – $Y] base, plus [equity / bonus]
<strong>Level:</strong> [Mid-level / Senior]</p>
<h3>About the role</h3>
<p>[Company] builds [product] for [users]. Our interface is [the primary surface customers use / the thing customers judge us on], and we&#39;re hiring a frontend developer to own [the specific area — the new dashboard, the design system, the customer-facing app].</p>
<p>You&#39;ll work closely with [n] designers and [n] backend engineers. [State plainly whether frontend here means product engineering, design-system work, or both.]</p>
<h3>What you&#39;ll do</h3>
<ul>
<li>Build user-facing features in [framework], from design handoff through production.</li>
<li>Turn design files into interfaces that match the intent — and push back when something won&#39;t work in a real browser.</li>
<li>Own [the design system / component library], keeping it consistent and documented.</li>
<li>Improve performance and accessibility across [the app] — we care about both and measure them.</li>
<li>Work with backend engineers on API shape rather than accepting whatever gets returned.</li>
</ul>
<h3>What we&#39;re looking for</h3>
<ul>
<li>[n]+ years building production frontends.</li>
<li>Deep [JavaScript / TypeScript] fundamentals — not just framework familiarity.</li>
<li>Solid [React / Vue / Svelte] experience, or the ability to demonstrate equivalent depth elsewhere.</li>
<li>Real CSS ability: layout, responsive design, and comfort debugging cross-browser issues.</li>
<li>Working knowledge of accessibility — semantic HTML, keyboard navigation, ARIA where appropriate.</li>
<li>An eye for detail. You notice when spacing is off by four pixels and you care.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Nice to have</h3>
<ul>
<li>Experience with [your build tooling / SSR framework / testing library].</li>
<li>Performance work: bundle size, Core Web Vitals, rendering.</li>
<li>Design ability, or a track record of working closely with designers.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Our stack</h3>
<p>[Framework, language, styling approach, build tool, testing setup, hosting.] Be specific about styling — &quot;we use Tailwind&quot; and &quot;we use CSS-in-JS&quot; attract different people.</p>
<h3>Interview process</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>[30 min]</strong> Intro call.</li>
<li><strong>[60 min]</strong> Walk us through frontend work you&#39;ve built — decisions, trade-offs, what you&#39;d change.</li>
<li><strong>[90 min]</strong> Practical session: build or extend a small interface, either paired with us or as a paid exercise.</li>
<li><strong>[45 min]</strong> Meet the design and engineering team.</li>
</ol>
<h3>Benefits and how to apply</h3>
<p>[Benefits.] [Application link.] Send anything you&#39;ve built that we can look at — a live site, a component library, a side project. We read every application.</p>
<p>[Company] is an equal opportunity employer.</p>
<h2>Hiring frontend engineers: what&#39;s different</h2>
<h3>Ask for things you can look at</h3>
<p>Frontend is the one specialty where the work is publicly inspectable. A live URL tells you more in two minutes than an hour of questions: does it work on a phone, does it survive keyboard navigation, does it load quickly, does it fall apart at 320 pixels wide? Always invite links, and always open them.</p>
<h3>Screen for fundamentals, not framework trivia</h3>
<p>Framework knowledge is learnable in weeks; the underlying skills take years. Weight your evaluation toward the things that transfer:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>CSS and layout ability</strong> — still the most consistently underrated frontend skill, and the most common real-world gap.</li>
<li><strong>Browser fundamentals</strong> — the event loop, rendering, network waterfalls, why something is slow.</li>
<li><strong>Accessibility instincts</strong> — whether they reach for a semantic element before a <code>div</code> with a click handler.</li>
<li><strong>Judgment about state</strong> — knowing when something needs a global store and when it obviously doesn&#39;t.</li>
</ul>
<p>An engineer strong in all four will be productive in your framework within a month. The reverse is not true.</p>
<h3>Describe the design relationship honestly</h3>
<p>This is the detail experienced frontend candidates care most about and the one job posts almost always omit. There&#39;s a wide gap between &quot;designers hand over finished files and you implement them,&quot; &quot;you and a designer figure it out together,&quot; and &quot;there&#39;s no designer and you&#39;ll be making these calls.&quot; All three are legitimate roles. Only one of them matches any given candidate. Say which it is.</p>
<h3>Don&#39;t disguise a full-stack role</h3>
<p>If the job is sixty percent backend, saying so costs you a few applications and saves you a resignation at month five. Candidates who applied to build interfaces and ended up writing services do not stay.</p>
<h2>What to delete</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>A list of ten frameworks.</strong> Nobody is expert in all of them, and asking signals you don&#39;t know what you need.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Pixel-perfect&quot;</strong> paired with an impossible deadline — candidates recognize the contradiction.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Must have an eye for design&quot;</strong> with no designer on staff and no design budget.</li>
<li><strong>jQuery in the requirements</strong> unless you genuinely mean it, in which case say why.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Great frontend hires are evaluated on fundamentals — CSS, browser behavior, accessibility, and taste — not framework checklists. Write the post to reflect that: name the specific surface they&#39;ll own, be honest about the design relationship and the backend split, publish the range, and invite links to real work. Then actually go and look at what they send.</p>
