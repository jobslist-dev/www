3:Te55,<p>You worked hard to hire a great developer. Then what? The first 90 days decide whether that hire becomes a productive, committed team member — or a frustrated one who quietly starts looking again. Onboarding is the most overlooked, highest-ROI part of hiring. Here&#39;s a plan that works.</p>
<h2>Why onboarding matters more than you think</h2>
<p>A structured onboarding experience dramatically improves both productivity and retention. New hires who feel lost in their first weeks form a quiet doubt that&#39;s hard to reverse. New hires who feel supported and effective become advocates. The cost of getting this wrong is the entire cost of the hire — twice.</p>
<h2>Before day one</h2>
<p>The best onboarding starts before the new hire arrives:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Have everything ready.</strong> Accounts, hardware, access, and a working dev environment. Nothing says &quot;we&#39;re disorganized&quot; like spending day one filing IT tickets.</li>
<li><strong>Write the first week down.</strong> A simple plan they can follow removes anxiety.</li>
<li><strong>Tell the team.</strong> Make sure everyone knows who&#39;s joining and who&#39;s helping them settle in.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Days 1–30: Orient and contribute</h2>
<p>The goal of the first month is for the new hire to understand the landscape and ship something small.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Assign an onboarding buddy</strong> — a peer they can ask &quot;dumb&quot; questions without friction.</li>
<li><strong>Ship a small change in the first week.</strong> A tiny, real PR builds confidence and proves the pipeline works end to end.</li>
<li><strong>Document as they go.</strong> New eyes catch gaps in your docs — have them improve the onboarding guide for the next person.</li>
<li><strong>Set clear, small goals.</strong> Early wins matter more than big ones.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Days 31–60: Build momentum</h2>
<p>By the second month, they should be taking on real work with less hand-holding.</p>
<ul>
<li>Give them <strong>ownership of a feature or area</strong>, with support available.</li>
<li>Establish a <strong>regular 1:1</strong> to surface blockers and give feedback early.</li>
<li>Introduce them to the <strong>wider context</strong> — how the team&#39;s work connects to the business and the users.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Days 61–90: Full contribution</h2>
<p>By 90 days, a well-onboarded developer should be operating independently.</p>
<ul>
<li>They should be <strong>shipping meaningful work</strong> at a normal cadence.</li>
<li>They should understand the <strong>codebase, the team&#39;s norms, and the priorities</strong>.</li>
<li>Run a <strong>check-in conversation</strong>: how&#39;s it going, what&#39;s unclear, what would make them more effective?</li>
</ul>
<h2>Common onboarding mistakes</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Throwing them in with no plan</strong> — sink-or-swim wastes talent and breeds resentment.</li>
<li><strong>Overloading week one</strong> — pace the information; people can&#39;t absorb everything at once.</li>
<li><strong>No feedback loop</strong> — silence makes new hires anxious. Tell them how they&#39;re doing.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring the human side</strong> — belonging matters as much as productivity.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Onboarding is where hiring pays off — or doesn&#39;t. Prepare before day one, create early wins, ramp ownership deliberately over 90 days, and keep a tight feedback loop. Do that and your new hires become productive faster and stay far longer.</p>
