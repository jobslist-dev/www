3:Tc91,<p>For designers, the portfolio <em>is</em> the résumé. Hiring teams care less about a wall of polished screens and more about how you think — how you framed a problem, made decisions, and moved a metric. A portfolio that shows that gets interviews; a gallery of dribbble shots usually doesn&#39;t.</p>
<h2>Lead with case studies, not screenshots</h2>
<p>Three to four strong case studies beat twenty thumbnails. Each case study should tell a story:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>The problem</strong> — what were you solving, for whom, and why did it matter?</li>
<li><strong>Your role</strong> — what you specifically did (be honest on team projects).</li>
<li><strong>The process</strong> — research, explorations, constraints, the decisions you made and why.</li>
<li><strong>The outcome</strong> — shipped work and, ideally, impact (conversion lift, fewer support tickets, faster task completion).</li>
</ol>
<p>The &quot;why&quot; is the part juniors skip and seniors emphasize. Show the messy middle, not just the final comp.</p>
<h2>Show your thinking, including the cuts</h2>
<p>Hiring managers want to see judgment:</p>
<ul>
<li>Include <strong>alternatives you rejected</strong> and why — it proves you evaluate trade-offs.</li>
<li>Show <strong>before/after</strong> where you improved something existing.</li>
<li>A few <strong>process artifacts</strong> (sketches, flows, a wireframe) demonstrate range without cluttering.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Quality over quantity</h2>
<ul>
<li>Curate ruthlessly — your portfolio is judged by its <strong>weakest</strong> piece, so cut filler.</li>
<li>Tailor the order to the role: applying to a product team? Lead with product work, not the logo you designed.</li>
<li>If you&#39;re early-career, <strong>self-initiated or concept projects</strong> are fine — just treat them with the same case-study rigor.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Make it effortless to consume</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fast and simple</strong> — a slow, over-animated site frustrates busy reviewers.</li>
<li><strong>Readable</strong> — clear headings, short paragraphs; reviewers skim first.</li>
<li><strong>Mobile-friendly</strong> — people will open it on a phone.</li>
<li>Put your <strong>contact and résumé</strong> one click away.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Don&#39;t neglect the craft signals</h2>
<p>You&#39;re a designer — the portfolio itself is a work sample. Consistent spacing, type, and hierarchy quietly signal competence. But don&#39;t let visual polish hide thin thinking; substance first, then shine.</p>
<h2>Write like a human</h2>
<p>Skip the jargon (&quot;synergized cross-functional paradigms&quot;). Explain your work the way you&#39;d talk a teammate through it. Clear writing reads as clear thinking — and designers who can communicate are rare and valued.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A standout design portfolio is a small set of honest, well-told case studies that reveal how you think and what you shipped — fast to read, easy to skim, tailored to the role. Show the decisions, not just the pixels, and you&#39;ll get the call.</p>
