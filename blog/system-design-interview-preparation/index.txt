3:T1e2c,<p>System design is the round candidates most often prepare for incorrectly. The common approach — memorizing reference architectures for well-known products — produces someone who can recite a design but falls apart the moment the interviewer changes a requirement. That&#39;s the opposite of what&#39;s being tested.</p>
<p>The round is an assessment of how you handle an underspecified problem with someone watching. Prepare for that, and the architectures take care of themselves.</p>
<h2>What the interviewer is actually scoring</h2>
<p>Most rubrics come down to five things:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Do you clarify before you build?</strong> Candidates who start drawing boxes in minute one score badly, however good the boxes are.</li>
<li><strong>Can you make a reasonable design and justify it?</strong> Not the optimal design. A defensible one.</li>
<li><strong>Do you understand trade-offs?</strong> Every choice costs something. Naming the cost is the signal.</li>
<li><strong>Can you go deep when asked?</strong> Breadth first, then depth on demand.</li>
<li><strong>Are you someone to design with?</strong> Do you take input, or defend your first idea to the end?</li>
</ul>
<p>Notice that &quot;arrived at the same architecture the interviewer had in mind&quot; is not on the list.</p>
<h2>A structure for the hour</h2>
<p>Having a repeatable structure means you never freeze. Roughly:</p>
<h3>Requirements — 5 to 10 minutes</h3>
<p>Do not skip this, and do not rush it. Ask until you have:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Functional scope.</strong> What must it do? Push for a small, explicit list, then confirm it: &quot;so v1 is these three things — anything else critical?&quot;</li>
<li><strong>Scale.</strong> Users, requests per second, data volume, read/write ratio. If they say &quot;you decide,&quot; pick a number out loud and design for it.</li>
<li><strong>Constraints that change the design.</strong> Latency targets, consistency requirements, regional distribution, cost sensitivity.</li>
<li><strong>What&#39;s explicitly out of scope.</strong> Getting permission to ignore auth or billing saves ten minutes you&#39;ll want later.</li>
</ul>
<p>Write the agreed scope where you both can see it. Interviewers routinely score this section on its own.</p>
<h3>Back-of-envelope estimates — 5 minutes</h3>
<p>Rough numbers, said out loud. Daily active users, requests per second at peak, storage per year, bandwidth. Nobody checks your arithmetic; they check whether your design is proportionate to the load. A design for a million requests per second when the answer is fifty is itself a mistake.</p>
<h3>High-level design — 10 to 15 minutes</h3>
<p>Draw the main components and the flow between them: clients, API layer, services, data stores, queues, caches. Keep it coarse. Walk through one complete request end to end so the interviewer can follow your model, then ask: &quot;does this look reasonable before I go deeper?&quot;</p>
<p>That question is worth a lot. It shows collaboration and it stops you spending twenty minutes down a path they didn&#39;t want.</p>
<h3>Deep dive — 15 to 20 minutes</h3>
<p>The interviewer will usually pick the area. If they don&#39;t, choose the part that&#39;s actually hard — the data model, the consistency problem, the fan-out, the thing that breaks at scale. This is where you demonstrate depth: schema choices, indexing, partitioning, caching strategy and invalidation, queue semantics, idempotency.</p>
<h3>Bottlenecks and failure — 5 to 10 minutes</h3>
<p>Strong candidates volunteer this rather than waiting to be asked:</p>
<ul>
<li>Where does this break first as load grows?</li>
<li>What happens when each component fails? What&#39;s the blast radius?</li>
<li>Where&#39;s the single point of failure?</li>
<li>What would you monitor, and what would page someone?</li>
<li>What&#39;s the migration or rollout path?</li>
</ul>
<h2>What to actually study</h2>
<p>You need a working understanding — not memorized diagrams — of the components you&#39;ll assemble:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Databases.</strong> Relational vs document vs key-value, and honestly <em>when</em> each is right. Indexing, transactions, replication, sharding, and what you lose with each.</li>
<li><strong>Caching.</strong> Where to put it, invalidation strategies, and what happens on a cold cache or a stampede.</li>
<li><strong>Queues and async processing.</strong> At-least-once vs exactly-once, ordering, idempotency, dead letters, backpressure.</li>
<li><strong>Load balancing and horizontal scaling</strong>, including statelessness and session handling.</li>
<li><strong>Consistency models.</strong> Strong vs eventual, and the practical consequences of each for users.</li>
<li><strong>Failure and resilience.</strong> Timeouts, retries with backoff, circuit breakers, graceful degradation.</li>
</ul>
<p>For each, be able to say what it&#39;s for, what it costs, and when you&#39;d choose something else. That last part is the difference between a mid-level and a senior answer.</p>
<h2>Practice properly</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Say it out loud.</strong> Designing silently in your head builds none of the skill being tested. Talk to a whiteboard, a friend, or a recording.</li>
<li><strong>Time yourself.</strong> The hour goes faster than you think; running out before failure modes is a common way to lose points.</li>
<li><strong>Redesign systems you&#39;ve actually worked on.</strong> Familiar constraints make the practice concrete.</li>
<li><strong>Have someone change a requirement mid-flow</strong> — &quot;now it&#39;s global,&quot; &quot;now writes are ten times reads.&quot; Adapting gracefully is a large part of the score.</li>
<li><strong>Practise the specific area you avoid.</strong> Most people have one — usually data modeling or consistency. That&#39;s exactly where the deep dive will land.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Common ways candidates lose</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Designing before clarifying.</strong> The most frequent and most costly error.</li>
<li><strong>Over-engineering.</strong> Reaching for microservices, sharding, and a message bus for a system with modest load signals poor judgment, not sophistication.</li>
<li><strong>Presenting a memorized architecture</strong> and being unable to justify any part of it.</li>
<li><strong>Silence.</strong> An unnarrated five-minute think looks like being stuck. Say what you&#39;re weighing.</li>
<li><strong>Defending the first idea.</strong> Interviewer pushback is usually an invitation, not an attack. &quot;That&#39;s a fair point — the trade-off there is…&quot; scores well.</li>
<li><strong>Never mentioning what could go wrong.</strong> Failure thinking is what separates experienced engineers.</li>
</ul>
<h2>If you don&#39;t know something</h2>
<p>Say so, then reason from what you do know. &quot;I haven&#39;t used that in production, but I&#39;d expect the trade-off to be X, so here&#39;s how I&#39;d approach it&quot; is a strong answer. Bluffing is the only genuinely bad option — interviewers detect it immediately and it costs you far more than the gap ever would.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>System design interviews test your process, not your recall. Clarify requirements before drawing anything, size the problem out loud, keep the first pass coarse, go deep where it&#39;s hard, and volunteer failure modes before you&#39;re asked. Study components for their trade-offs rather than memorizing architectures — then practice out loud, against the clock, with someone changing the requirements on you.</p>
