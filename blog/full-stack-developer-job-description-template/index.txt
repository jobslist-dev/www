3:T1ac9,<p>&quot;Full-stack developer&quot; is the least precise title in engineering. At one company it means a product engineer comfortable across the whole request path. At another it means one person expected to do frontend, backend, infrastructure, database administration, and design — a job posting that experienced developers recognize instantly and skip.</p>
<p>The difference between those two posts is a paragraph of honesty. Here&#39;s a template that writes it.</p>
<h2>The template</h2>
<h3>Full-Stack Developer at [Company]</h3>
<p><strong>Location:</strong> [City / Remote — [region] / Hybrid]
<strong>Compensation:</strong> [$X – $Y] base, plus [equity / bonus]
<strong>Level:</strong> [Mid-level / Senior]</p>
<h3>About the role</h3>
<p>[Company] builds [product] for [users]. We&#39;re a team of [n] engineers who work across the stack rather than splitting into frontend and backend teams.</p>
<p>We&#39;re hiring a full-stack developer to own [a specific product area] end to end — from the interface a customer touches through to the data model behind it.</p>
<p><strong>What full-stack means here:</strong> [Be precise. For example: &quot;roughly 60% frontend in React, 40% backend in Node. You will not be managing infrastructure — we have a platform team for that.&quot;] This one line will do more for your application quality than the rest of the post.</p>
<h3>What you&#39;ll do</h3>
<ul>
<li>Ship complete features: interface, API, data model, and the tests around them.</li>
<li>Work directly with [design / product / customers] to shape what gets built, not just implement tickets.</li>
<li>Own [a product area] in production, including the parts that break.</li>
<li>Make deliberate decisions about where complexity should live — client, server, or database.</li>
<li>Review code across the stack and help keep the codebase coherent.</li>
</ul>
<h3>What we&#39;re looking for</h3>
<ul>
<li>[n]+ years shipping production web applications end to end.</li>
<li>Solid experience with [your frontend framework] and [your backend language].</li>
<li>Real database competence — you can design a schema and work out why a query is slow.</li>
<li>Product instincts. You ask what a feature is for before deciding how to build it.</li>
<li>Comfort with ambiguity and the judgment to know when something is good enough to ship.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Nice to have</h3>
<ul>
<li>[Your specific stack extras: TypeScript, a particular ORM, testing tools.]</li>
<li>Experience in [your domain].</li>
<li>Some infrastructure familiarity — helpful, not required.</li>
</ul>
<h3>What this role is not</h3>
<p>[Delete this section if it doesn&#39;t apply, but most companies need it.] This is not a DevOps role, and it&#39;s not a design role. [Name who owns those.]</p>
<h3>Our stack</h3>
<p>[Frontend framework, backend language, database, hosting, deployment.] You don&#39;t need every item.</p>
<h3>Interview process</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>[30 min]</strong> Intro call.</li>
<li><strong>[60 min]</strong> Walk through a feature you built end to end — decisions on both sides of the API.</li>
<li><strong>[90 min]</strong> Practical session: extend a small full-stack app with us, or a paid take-home.</li>
<li><strong>[45 min]</strong> Meet the team.</li>
</ol>
<h3>Benefits and how to apply</h3>
<p>[Benefits.] [Application link.] We reply to every applicant.</p>
<p>[Company] is an equal opportunity employer.</p>
<h2>Hiring full-stack: what&#39;s different</h2>
<h3>Define the ratio, in writing</h3>
<p>Full-stack candidates have wildly different centres of gravity. Someone who is 80% frontend and someone who is 80% backend will both apply to your post, and both will call themselves full-stack honestly.</p>
<p>Stating the split — even approximately — lets the right people self-select and stops you interviewing four candidates whose strengths sit on the wrong side. It also protects you from the most common full-stack hiring failure: hiring someone strong on one side, assigning them mostly to the other, and losing them within a year.</p>
<h3>Don&#39;t use &quot;full-stack&quot; to mean &quot;cheaper than two hires&quot;</h3>
<p>Experienced developers can spot this instantly. The tell is a requirements list spanning React, Node, Kubernetes, Postgres administration, CI/CD, mobile, and &quot;an eye for design.&quot; Posts like this attract inexperienced candidates and repel everyone else, which is the exact inverse of what you want.</p>
<p>If you genuinely need one person to cover an enormous surface because you&#39;re small, say that plainly and frame it as ownership: &quot;You&#39;ll be our second engineer, so you&#39;ll touch everything. Here&#39;s what that actually looks like day to day.&quot; Honest breadth is attractive to a certain kind of engineer. Disguised understaffing is not.</p>
<h3>Screen for depth somewhere</h3>
<p>The failure mode of full-stack hiring is a candidate who is shallow everywhere. Guard against it by picking one side — their stronger side — and going genuinely deep in the interview. You&#39;re checking that they have the capacity for depth, not that they&#39;ve achieved it in every layer.</p>
<p>A good probe: <strong>&quot;Tell me about a time the right fix was on the other side of the stack from where the bug appeared.&quot;</strong> Real full-stack engineers have several of these stories, and telling one demonstrates the end-to-end mental model you&#39;re actually hiring for.</p>
<h3>Value the API boundary</h3>
<p>The distinctive advantage of a full-stack engineer is that they design both sides of an interface at once. Ask how they&#39;ve shaped an API for the client that consumes it, or when they&#39;ve moved logic between client and server and why. Candidates who think carefully about that boundary are the ones who make small teams fast.</p>
<h2>What to delete</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>A requirements list spanning more than two layers of specialist tooling.</strong></li>
<li><strong>&quot;Wear many hats&quot;</strong> with no specifics.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Full-stack&quot; in the title when the job is 90% one side.</strong> Title it accurately and you&#39;ll get better applicants.</li>
<li><strong>Design responsibilities</strong> unless you&#39;re paying for a designer&#39;s skills too.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>The whole job of a full-stack post is defining the term. State the rough split between frontend and backend, name what the role explicitly does not cover, and describe breadth as ownership rather than as savings. Then interview for depth on one side and for end-to-end judgment across the boundary — that combination is what actually makes a full-stack hire valuable.</p>
