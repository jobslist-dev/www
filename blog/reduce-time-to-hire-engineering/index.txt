3:Td1a,<p>Speed is a feature in hiring. The best developers are often off the market within a couple of weeks, and every day a role sits open is a day the work isn&#39;t getting done. Yet many teams equate &quot;thorough&quot; with &quot;slow.&quot; You don&#39;t have to choose — a fast process can also be a rigorous one. The trick is removing waste, not removing rigor.</p>
<h2>Why speed matters</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>You lose candidates to faster competitors.</strong> Strong people interview at several places. The company that decides first often wins.</li>
<li><strong>Open roles cost money every day.</strong> Work piles up or burns out the existing team.</li>
<li><strong>Momentum compounds.</strong> A candidate who feels a process is crisp and respectful is more likely to say yes.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Find the bottlenecks</h2>
<p>Most slow processes aren&#39;t slow because of one big delay — they&#39;re slow because of many small ones. Common culprits:</p>
<ul>
<li>Days lost <strong>scheduling</strong> each round.</li>
<li>A <strong>hiring manager</strong> who takes a week to review submissions.</li>
<li><strong>Too many rounds</strong>, each adding scheduling and decision lag.</li>
<li><strong>Slow debriefs</strong> where feedback trickles in over days.</li>
</ul>
<p>Measure where days actually go, then attack the biggest gaps.</p>
<h2>Tactics that cut time without cutting quality</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Compress the loop.</strong> Most roles need one screen, one realistic technical exercise, and one team conversation. Extra rounds rarely add signal — they add delay.</li>
<li><strong>Batch and pre-book interviews.</strong> Reserve interviewer slots in advance so scheduling isn&#39;t a bottleneck.</li>
<li><strong>Decide fast after each stage.</strong> Set an SLA: feedback within 24 hours, decisions within 48.</li>
<li><strong>Start with a higher-fit pool.</strong> The biggest time sink is screening unqualified applicants. Posting where developers actually are means fewer wrong-fit résumés and faster shortlists.</li>
<li><strong>Prepare the offer early.</strong> Know your range and approval path before the final interview so you can move the moment you decide.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Don&#39;t sacrifice the things that matter</h2>
<p>Speed should come from removing waste, not from skipping diligence:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep your <strong>work-sample assessment</strong> — it&#39;s your best predictor.</li>
<li>Keep a <strong>structured rubric</strong> — it keeps fast decisions accurate.</li>
<li>Keep <strong>candidate communication</strong> prompt — speed and respect reinforce each other.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Communicate the timeline</h2>
<p>Tell candidates your process and timeline up front. It sets expectations, signals that you&#39;re organized, and keeps them engaged rather than drifting toward another offer while they wait in silence.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Reducing time-to-hire is mostly about eliminating dead time — scheduling lag, slow debriefs, unnecessary rounds — and starting from a higher-quality pool. Keep the rigor that predicts success, cut the waste that doesn&#39;t, and you&#39;ll win more of the candidates you want while keeping your standards intact.</p>
