3:T1c86,<p>Most developers treat their professional profile as an obligation — a CV pasted into a form, updated reluctantly when a job search starts. That&#39;s a missed opportunity, because a profile works differently from a CV. A CV is read by someone who already has it. A profile has to be <em>found</em> first, then survive a ten-second skim by someone deciding whether to open it.</p>
<p>Optimising for those two jobs is straightforward, and almost nobody does it.</p>
<h2>First, get found: profiles are search results</h2>
<p>Recruiters and hiring managers find candidates by searching for specific terms — technologies, titles, locations, seniority. If those words don&#39;t appear in your profile, you don&#39;t exist for that search, no matter how good you are.</p>
<p>So make sure your profile actually contains:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The technologies you work in, named exactly.</strong> &quot;React,&quot; &quot;PostgreSQL,&quot; &quot;Kubernetes&quot; — the specific words someone would type. Vague phrasing like &quot;modern JavaScript frameworks&quot; matches nothing.</li>
<li><strong>Your role title in recognisable form.</strong> If your company calls you a &quot;Software Craftsperson III,&quot; also include &quot;Backend Engineer&quot; somewhere in the text. Nobody searches for internal titles.</li>
<li><strong>Seniority language</strong> — senior, staff, lead — if it applies.</li>
<li><strong>Location and remote preference.</strong> A significant share of searches filter on this.</li>
</ul>
<p>Spread these naturally through your headline, summary, and role descriptions. This isn&#39;t keyword stuffing; it&#39;s making sure the words you&#39;d want to be found by are actually present.</p>
<h2>The headline is the highest-value text you own</h2>
<p>It appears in every search result, next to your name, and it&#39;s the main thing people use to decide whether to click. The default — your current job title at your current company — wastes it.</p>
<p>A better structure names what you do, your main technologies, and optionally what you&#39;re looking for:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Senior Backend Engineer · Go, Postgres, distributed systems · Open to remote roles</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Compare that to &quot;Software Engineer at Acme Corp,&quot; which tells a searcher nothing about whether you&#39;re relevant.</p>
<p>If you&#39;re actively looking, say so plainly. It reliably increases inbound contact, and there&#39;s no penalty for it.</p>
<h2>Write a summary someone will actually read</h2>
<p>Keep it to three short paragraphs, written in plain first person.</p>
<p><strong>What you do and what you&#39;re good at.</strong> Be specific about the kind of engineering, not just the title. &quot;I build data-heavy backends — mostly Go and Postgres, usually where correctness matters more than speed of delivery&quot; tells a reader far more than &quot;passionate engineer.&quot;</p>
<p><strong>Something concrete you&#39;ve done.</strong> One example with a result. This is the paragraph that converts a skim into a message.</p>
<p><strong>What you want next.</strong> The most-skipped and most useful part. Recruiters are trying to work out whether to contact you. Telling them — &quot;interested in remote backend roles at small teams; not looking for management&quot; — filters out irrelevant approaches and increases relevant ones.</p>
<p>Skip the third-person voice, the buzzword lists, and the mission statements. They read as filler.</p>
<h2>Make each role show impact, not duties</h2>
<p>The common failure is a list of responsibilities, which reads identically for every engineer at that level. Responsibilities describe the job; accomplishments describe you.</p>
<p>For each role, aim for three or four bullets covering:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Something you built</strong>, with the technology named and the outcome stated.</li>
<li><strong>A problem you fixed</strong>, with a before and after — latency, cost, reliability, delivery time.</li>
<li><strong>Scope signals</strong>: team size, system scale, what you owned.</li>
<li><strong>Anything beyond writing code</strong>: mentoring, hiring, architecture decisions, cross-team work.</li>
</ul>
<p>Use real numbers where you honestly have them, and skip them where you don&#39;t rather than inventing something. &quot;Cut p95 latency from 800ms to 120ms&quot; is worth ten lines of adjectives.</p>
<h2>Details that quietly matter</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>A real photo.</strong> Profiles with one get substantially more engagement. It doesn&#39;t need to be professional — just clear and recent.</li>
<li><strong>Complete the basics.</strong> Location, current role with accurate dates, and a link to your best work.</li>
<li><strong>Link to your code and your site</strong>, not only to your employer.</li>
<li><strong>List skills honestly</strong> and order them so the ones you actually want to be hired for sit at the top.</li>
<li><strong>Keep dates truthful.</strong> Gaps are fine and common; inconsistencies with your CV are not.</li>
<li><strong>Write in the language of your target market</strong>, if you&#39;re searching internationally.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Signals that you&#39;re a real engineer, not just a listing</h2>
<p>The profiles that generate the best inbound aren&#39;t the most polished — they&#39;re the ones showing a person who does things:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Occasional posts about your actual work.</strong> A short note on a problem you solved is worth more than any amount of curated content. You don&#39;t need an audience; hiring managers who land on your profile will read it.</li>
<li><strong>Links to things you&#39;ve made.</strong> Projects, talks, writing.</li>
<li><strong>Meaningful contributions to conversations</strong> in your technical area.</li>
</ul>
<p>Consistency matters more than volume. Two thoughtful posts a year beats a burst of activity in the month you start job hunting, which is transparent to everyone.</p>
<h2>If you don&#39;t want to be contacted constantly</h2>
<p>Plenty of developers dislike recruiter volume. You can shape it rather than opting out:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Be specific about what you want.</strong> Vague profiles attract scattergun outreach; precise ones attract fewer and better messages.</li>
<li><strong>State what you&#39;re not looking for.</strong> &quot;Not interested in relocation&quot; or &quot;not looking for contract work&quot; is respected more often than you&#39;d expect.</li>
<li><strong>Keep the profile current even when settled.</strong> A profile updated in an emergency is always weaker than one maintained calmly — and the best opportunities tend to arrive when you&#39;re not looking.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Your profile has two jobs: appear in the right searches, and survive a ten-second skim. Name your technologies explicitly, replace the default headline with what you actually do, write a short human summary that ends with what you want next, and turn responsibilities into outcomes. An hour of work here changes the quality of everything that arrives in your inbox for the next year.</p>
