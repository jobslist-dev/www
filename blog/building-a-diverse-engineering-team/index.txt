3:Te17,<p>Diverse engineering teams build better products. Different backgrounds surface different edge cases, challenge assumptions, and reflect the real range of people who use what you build. But diversity doesn&#39;t happen by accident or by good intentions alone — it comes from deliberately removing the bias baked into typical hiring. Here are changes that actually work.</p>
<h2>Start with how you write the role</h2>
<p>The job description is the first filter, and a careless one screens people out before they ever apply:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Trim the requirements.</strong> Long &quot;must-have&quot; lists disproportionately deter qualified candidates from underrepresented groups, who are more likely to self-select out unless they meet every item.</li>
<li><strong>Watch the language.</strong> Avoid jargon and clichés that signal a narrow culture (&quot;work hard, play hard,&quot; &quot;we&#39;re a family&quot;).</li>
<li><strong>State the salary range.</strong> Pay transparency reduces the negotiation gaps that entrench inequity.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Widen where you source</h2>
<p>If you always recruit from the same few channels, you&#39;ll always get the same few profiles. Broaden your reach:</p>
<ul>
<li>Post where a <strong>broad, technical audience</strong> gathers rather than relying solely on referrals, which tend to replicate the existing team.</li>
<li>Value <strong>non-traditional paths</strong> — bootcamp grads, career changers, and self-taught engineers bring real skill and motivation.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Make the process structured</h2>
<p>Unstructured interviews are where bias does the most damage, because they let &quot;gut feeling&quot; — really, similarity to the interviewer — drive decisions. The fixes are well established:</p>
<ol>
<li>Ask every candidate the <strong>same questions</strong>.</li>
<li>Score against a <strong>rubric</strong>, not a vibe.</li>
<li>Use <strong>work-sample tests</strong> that measure ability directly.</li>
<li>Have interviewers record assessments <strong>independently</strong> before debriefing.</li>
</ol>
<p>These steps improve accuracy <em>and</em> fairness at the same time.</p>
<h2>Reduce identity signals where you can</h2>
<p>Early in the process, focus on the work. Evaluating a take-home or a code sample on its merits — before names, schools, and logos color the judgment — helps surface talent you might otherwise overlook.</p>
<h2>Build an inclusive candidate experience</h2>
<p>Candidates notice signals about whether they&#39;ll belong:</p>
<ul>
<li>Show a <strong>real, varied team</strong> on your company page.</li>
<li>Offer <strong>flexibility</strong> (remote options, reasonable scheduling) that widens who can participate.</li>
<li>Communicate respectfully and promptly with everyone.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Measure and keep going</h2>
<p>Diversity isn&#39;t a one-time campaign. Track where candidates drop out of your funnel, look for stages where certain groups fall off, and fix the process — not the people. And remember that hiring is only the start; retention and inclusion determine whether a diverse team actually thrives.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Building a diverse engineering team is mostly about removing bias from a normal hiring process: write inclusive role descriptions, source broadly, structure your interviews, and judge the work on its merits. These changes make your hiring fairer and more accurate — better teams, better decisions, better products.</p>
