3:T190e,<p>Most software engineer job descriptions are assembled from whatever the last one said, which is why most of them read identically and convert poorly. Below is a complete template you can copy and adapt in about fifteen minutes, followed by notes on what to change, what to cut, and why each section is there.</p>
<h2>The template</h2>
<p>Copy everything from here to the end of the section, then replace the bracketed parts.</p>
<h3>Software Engineer at [Company]</h3>
<p><strong>Location:</strong> [City, or &quot;Remote — [region]&quot;, or &quot;Hybrid, [n] days in [city]&quot;]
<strong>Compensation:</strong> [$X – $Y] base, plus [equity / bonus / benefits summary]
<strong>Level:</strong> [Mid-level / Senior / Staff]</p>
<h3>About us</h3>
<p>[Company] builds [what you build] for [who uses it]. We&#39;re a team of [n] people, [n] of them engineers, and we&#39;re [funded / profitable / bootstrapped]. [One concrete sentence about traction — customers, scale, or growth.]</p>
<p>We&#39;re hiring a [level] software engineer to [the single most important outcome this person owns].</p>
<h3>What you&#39;ll do</h3>
<ul>
<li>Build and ship features across [the parts of the product this role touches].</li>
<li>Own [a specific system, service, or area] end to end — design, implementation, testing, and production.</li>
<li>Work directly with [design / product / customers] to turn problems into working software.</li>
<li>Review code, write tests, and help keep our [codebase / deployment pipeline] healthy.</li>
<li>Participate in [on-call rotation / incident response], which currently means [an honest description of the load].</li>
</ul>
<h3>What we&#39;re looking for</h3>
<ul>
<li>[n]+ years building production software, in any language.</li>
<li>Strong experience with [1–2 core technologies you genuinely need].</li>
<li>Comfort owning work with limited supervision — you can take an ambiguous problem, ask the right questions, and deliver.</li>
<li>Clear written communication; we make most decisions in writing.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Nice to have</h3>
<ul>
<li>Experience with [adjacent technology].</li>
<li>Background in [your domain].</li>
<li>Open-source contributions, side projects, or anything you can show us.</li>
</ul>
<p>We don&#39;t expect every box ticked. If you meet most of this and the work sounds interesting, apply.</p>
<h3>Our stack</h3>
<p>[List the actual stack: language, framework, database, infrastructure.] You don&#39;t need experience in all of it.</p>
<h3>Interview process</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>[30 min]</strong> Intro call with [name / role].</li>
<li><strong>[60 min]</strong> Technical conversation about your past work — no whiteboard puzzles.</li>
<li><strong>[90 min]</strong> Practical session: [pairing on a realistic problem / walkthrough of a take-home you&#39;re paid for].</li>
<li><strong>[45 min]</strong> Meet the team and ask us anything.</li>
</ol>
<p>We aim to go from application to decision in [n] working days and will tell you where you stand at every stage.</p>
<h3>Benefits</h3>
<p>[Health cover, PTO policy with the actual number, remote/equipment budget, learning budget, parental leave, pension/401k.]</p>
<h3>How to apply</h3>
<p>[Application link.] [Any specific instruction — e.g. &quot;tell us about something you built and what you&#39;d change about it.&quot;] We read every application and reply to everyone.</p>
<p>[Company] is an equal opportunity employer. We welcome applicants from every background.</p>
<h2>What to change first</h2>
<p>The template is a skeleton. Four fields do most of the work:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The compensation range.</strong> The single highest-impact line. Candidates increasingly filter out posts without one, and publishing it removes the misalignment that kills offers in the final week.</li>
<li><strong>The opening outcome sentence.</strong> &quot;To own our billing system&#39;s move off the monolith&quot; beats &quot;to join our fast-paced team&quot; by an enormous margin. Name the actual work.</li>
<li><strong>The years-of-experience number.</strong> Inflated requirements filter out capable people and attract nobody extra. If a strong three-year engineer could do this job, don&#39;t write eight.</li>
<li><strong>The interview process.</strong> Publishing it is rare enough to be a differentiator, and it pre-empts the top question every candidate has.</li>
</ul>
<h2>What to delete</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>&quot;Rockstar,&quot; &quot;ninja,&quot; &quot;wizard,&quot; &quot;guru.&quot;</strong> These read as dated at best.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Fast-paced environment.&quot;</strong> Every candidate reads this as &quot;understaffed and disorganized.&quot;</li>
<li><strong>&quot;We&#39;re like a family.&quot;</strong> Families don&#39;t run layoffs, and experienced candidates know it.</li>
<li><strong>A requirements list longer than eight items.</strong> Long lists don&#39;t raise quality; they suppress applications, disproportionately from candidates who self-assess conservatively.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Competitive salary.&quot;</strong> It communicates nothing and signals you&#39;d rather not say.</li>
<li><strong>Unpaid multi-day take-homes.</strong> Mention one and you lose most senior candidates before the first call.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Why the structure works</h2>
<p>Candidates skim job posts in well under a minute, in a predictable order: title, location, money, then what the job actually is. The template front-loads exactly that. Everything a candidate needs in order to decide <em>whether to keep reading</em> appears before they scroll.</p>
<p>The second half — stack, process, benefits, how to apply — is for the candidate who has already decided they&#39;re interested and is now looking for reasons to disqualify you. Give them straight answers there and you keep the people most likely to have other options.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A good job description isn&#39;t a legal document or a wish list; it&#39;s a sales page for a specific piece of work. Lead with the outcome and the number, keep requirements honest and short, publish your process, and delete every phrase that appears in a thousand other posts. Fifteen minutes of editing here changes the quality of every application that follows.</p>
