3:Teb3,<p>Your résumé has one job: get you the interview. It won&#39;t get you hired — that&#39;s what the interview is for — so it needs to do that single thing well. Most developer résumés fail because they list duties instead of impact and bury the good parts. Here&#39;s how to fix yours.</p>
<h2>Lead with impact, not responsibilities</h2>
<p>The biggest upgrade you can make is turning &quot;responsible for X&quot; into &quot;achieved Y by doing X.&quot; Hiring managers skim for results.</p>
<ul>
<li>❌ &quot;Responsible for the backend API.&quot;</li>
<li>✅ &quot;Cut API p95 latency 40% by redesigning the caching layer, saving ~$3k/mo in infra.&quot;</li>
</ul>
<p>Use the pattern <strong>[action verb] + [what you did] + [measurable result]</strong>. Numbers — percentages, dollars, users, time saved — make you concrete and credible. If you don&#39;t have exact metrics, estimate honestly (&quot;~&quot;, &quot;roughly&quot;).</p>
<h2>Make it skimmable in 10 seconds</h2>
<p>Recruiters and engineers spend seconds on a first pass. Help them:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Clear sections</strong>: Summary (optional, 2 lines), Experience, Skills, Projects, Education.</li>
<li><strong>Reverse-chronological</strong> experience, most recent first.</li>
<li><strong>Bullets, not paragraphs</strong> — 3–5 per role, each starting with a strong verb.</li>
<li><strong>One page</strong> for most people; two only if you have 8+ years of relevant work.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Get through the filters without keyword-stuffing</h2>
<p>Many companies screen résumés (by software or a human checklist) against the job description. You don&#39;t need to game it — you need to mirror the real language:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use the <strong>exact terms</strong> from the posting where they&#39;re genuinely true of you (&quot;PostgreSQL&quot; not just &quot;SQL&quot; if that&#39;s what they asked for).</li>
<li>Keep a <strong>Skills</strong> section with your actual stack, grouped (Languages, Frameworks, Tools, Cloud).</li>
<li>Avoid tables, columns, and graphics that parsers choke on — a clean, single-column layout is safest.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Show real work</h2>
<p>For developers, evidence beats adjectives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Link your <strong>GitHub</strong>, <strong>portfolio</strong>, or a standout <strong>project</strong> with a one-line description of what it does and the stack.</li>
<li>For each project: the problem, your approach, the outcome. &quot;Built a real-time chat app (TypeScript, WebSockets) handling 5k concurrent users&quot; says more than &quot;proficient in TypeScript.&quot;</li>
</ul>
<h2>Tailor it (at least a little)</h2>
<p>You don&#39;t need a fresh résumé per application, but spend two minutes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reorder bullets so the most <strong>relevant</strong> experience is on top.</li>
<li>Adjust the summary line to match the role.</li>
<li>Drop anything irrelevant that dilutes the signal.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Common mistakes to cut</h2>
<ul>
<li>Objective statements (&quot;seeking a challenging role…&quot;) — wastes the top of the page.</li>
<li>A wall of every technology you&#39;ve touched once.</li>
<li>Typos and inconsistent tense/formatting — they read as carelessness.</li>
<li>Listing soft skills with no evidence (&quot;great communicator&quot;).</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A great developer résumé is skimmable, impact-driven, honest, and backed by real work. Lead with results and numbers, mirror the role&#39;s language, link to proof, and keep it tight. Do that and you&#39;ll convert far more applications into interviews.</p>
