3:Tde1,<p>Technical interviews feel high-stakes, but they reward preparation more than raw talent. The candidates who do well aren&#39;t always the strongest engineers — they&#39;re the ones who practiced the format, communicated clearly, and stayed calm. Here&#39;s how to get there.</p>
<h2>Understand the format before you study</h2>
<p>Ask your recruiter what to expect — it&#39;s a completely normal question and it shapes your prep:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Coding</strong> (live or take-home)?</li>
<li><strong>System design</strong> (usually mid/senior+)?</li>
<li><strong>Behavioral / values</strong>?</li>
<li>Languages or tools they expect?</li>
</ul>
<p>Preparing for the wrong format is the most common waste of effort.</p>
<h2>Practice coding the way you&#39;ll be tested</h2>
<p>If there&#39;s a live coding round:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Practice out loud.</strong> The interview tests your thinking, not just the answer. Narrate your approach as you go.</li>
<li><strong>Drill the fundamentals</strong> — arrays, strings, hash maps, recursion, basic trees/graphs. Most interviews don&#39;t need exotic algorithms.</li>
<li><strong>Do timed reps</strong> so the clock doesn&#39;t rattle you, but don&#39;t grind 500 problems — pattern recognition matters more than volume.</li>
<li><strong>Always clarify first.</strong> Restate the problem, ask about inputs and edge cases, then code. Jumping straight to typing is a red flag.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Prepare for system design (if applicable)</h2>
<p>For mid/senior roles, expect open-ended design (&quot;design a URL shortener&quot;):</p>
<ul>
<li>Start by <strong>clarifying requirements and scale</strong> — don&#39;t assume.</li>
<li>Talk through <strong>trade-offs</strong> out loud (consistency vs. availability, SQL vs. NoSQL) — they&#39;re testing judgment, not the &quot;right&quot; answer.</li>
<li>Sketch the <strong>major components</strong> (API, storage, caching, queues) and where bottlenecks would be.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Communication is half the grade</h2>
<p>Interviewers are evaluating what it&#39;s like to work with you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Think aloud, even when stuck — &quot;I&#39;m considering two approaches…&quot; beats silence.</li>
<li>If you&#39;re stuck, say so and reason through it; a good interviewer will nudge.</li>
<li>Be receptive to hints — treating them as collaboration, not failure, scores well.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Prepare your stories and your questions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Have 3–4 <strong>concrete stories</strong> ready (a hard bug, a disagreement, a project you&#39;re proud of) — see our behavioral-interview guide.</li>
<li>Prepare <strong>questions to ask them</strong>: how the team works, what success looks like in 90 days, the biggest current challenge. Good questions signal seniority — and the interview is your evaluation of them, too.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Logistics that calm the nerves</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Test your setup</strong> the day before (camera, mic, screen-share, editor).</li>
<li>Have water, a notepad, and a quiet space.</li>
<li>Get sleep — rested judgment beats one more practice problem.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Find out the format, practice out loud, clarify before you code, narrate your thinking, and come with stories and sharp questions. Preparation turns interview anxiety into a conversation you&#39;re ready for — and that&#39;s what gets offers.</p>
