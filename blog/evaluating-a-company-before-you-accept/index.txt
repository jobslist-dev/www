3:T13c2,<p>Job searching is exhausting, and by the time an offer arrives the temptation is to just say yes. But a bad job costs you a year of your career and the energy to escape it. The interview process is your only real window into a company while you still have leverage — and once you have an offer, you have the most leverage you&#39;ll ever have. Use it.</p>
<h2>Remember you&#39;re evaluating them too</h2>
<p>Interviews condition you into supplicant mode: prove yourself, be agreeable, don&#39;t rock the boat. That framing costs candidates dearly. A company that penalizes you for asking substantive questions has told you something important for free.</p>
<p>The good news is that diligence reads as seniority. Thoughtful questions about how work actually gets done make you look like someone who has been burned before and learned — which is exactly what experienced engineers look like.</p>
<h2>Ask questions that can&#39;t be answered with a slogan</h2>
<p>&quot;What&#39;s the culture like?&quot; produces nothing. Everyone says collaborative and fast-paced. Ask questions where the honest answer and the flattering answer are different:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>&quot;Walk me through the last thing that shipped late. What happened?&quot;</strong> Blame-first answers tell you how failure is handled here.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;How does someone get promoted on this team? Who was promoted most recently?&quot;</strong> Vagueness means there&#39;s no real path.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;What does on-call look like, and how often does it wake people up?&quot;</strong> A team that&#39;s honest about pager load is a team that manages it.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;How much of the codebase would you rewrite if you could?&quot;</strong> Everyone has debt; you&#39;re testing whether they can discuss it openly.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Why is this role open?&quot;</strong> Growth, backfill, or churn — each means something very different.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;What&#39;s the hardest part of working here?&quot;</strong> If nobody can name one, they&#39;re either not being honest or not paying attention.</li>
</ul>
<p>Ask several people the same question separately. Consistent answers mean a real culture. Wildly different ones mean each team is on its own.</p>
<h2>Read the engineering signals</h2>
<p>How a team builds tells you more than how it describes itself:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Deployment frequency.</strong> Teams that ship daily have solved problems that teams shipping quarterly haven&#39;t.</li>
<li><strong>Testing and review norms.</strong> Ask what happens to a PR from submission to production.</li>
<li><strong>Meeting load.</strong> Ask an engineer how many hours of focused time they had yesterday.</li>
<li><strong>Documentation.</strong> Ask how a new hire learns the system. &quot;You&#39;ll pick it up by asking people&quot; means the knowledge is trapped in heads.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Check the money and the runway</h2>
<p>This is not rude, it&#39;s basic diligence — and it matters most at smaller companies.</p>
<p>Ask directly: how is the company funded, what&#39;s the current runway, when was the last raise, is the team growing or flat? At a startup, ask how equity is structured, what the current valuation and strike price are, and what happens to unvested shares if you leave. If any of that is treated as an inappropriate question, that&#39;s your answer.</p>
<h2>Do your own reference check</h2>
<p>You&#39;ve been referenced; reference them back. Look up current and former engineers on professional networks and ask for fifteen minutes. Most people will take the call, and former employees are candid in ways interview panels can&#39;t be.</p>
<p>Ask them one thing: <strong>&quot;What do you wish you&#39;d known before you joined?&quot;</strong> It gets you further than a dozen careful questions.</p>
<h2>Red flags worth walking away from</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Pressure to decide immediately.</strong> Any offer worth taking survives a few days of thought. Urgency is a tactic.</li>
<li><strong>Refusal to put things in writing</strong> — comp, level, remote policy, start date.</li>
<li><strong>Disorganized, rescheduled, or contradictory interviews.</strong> This is the company at its best behavior. It does not improve after you join.</li>
<li><strong>Everyone in the reporting chain is new.</strong> Ask where the previous people went.</li>
<li><strong>Talking about the team as &quot;a family.&quot;</strong> Families don&#39;t run layoffs.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Accepting an offer is a bigger decision than any single interview answer you gave. Ask questions where the honest answer differs from the flattering one, read how the team actually builds and pays, backchannel with people who&#39;ve left, and treat pressure to sign fast as information. A week of diligence is cheap; a year in the wrong job isn&#39;t.</p>
