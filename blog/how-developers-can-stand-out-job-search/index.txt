3:Td76,<p>If you&#39;re a developer looking for your next role, the market can feel like a black hole — applications go in, nothing comes out. The good news: most applicants do the same generic things, so a little intentionality goes a long way. Here&#39;s how to stand out and find a role that actually fits.</p>
<h2>Build evidence, not just claims</h2>
<p>Anyone can list skills. Few can point to proof. The single best thing you can do is build visible evidence of your ability:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <strong>portfolio</strong> or a few <strong>side projects</strong> that solve real problems.</li>
<li>A <strong>GitHub</strong> with genuine commits and clear READMEs.</li>
<li>A short <strong>write-up</strong> of something interesting you built or debugged.</li>
</ul>
<p>You don&#39;t need a huge body of work — a couple of thoughtful projects beat a long list of unfinished ones.</p>
<h2>Make your profile do the work</h2>
<p>Whether it&#39;s a résumé or a marketplace profile, lead with impact, not duties:</p>
<ul>
<li>Replace &quot;responsible for the backend&quot; with &quot;cut API latency 40% by redesigning the caching layer.&quot;</li>
<li>Show <strong>outcomes and numbers</strong> where you can.</li>
<li>Keep it scannable — hiring managers spend seconds, not minutes.</li>
</ul>
<p>A rich, specific profile lets the right companies find <em>you</em>, which flips the dynamic in your favor.</p>
<h2>Apply with focus, not volume</h2>
<p>Blasting 200 generic applications is slower and less effective than 20 tailored ones. For roles you genuinely want:</p>
<ul>
<li>Read the listing closely and address what they actually need.</li>
<li>Mention something specific about the company or problem.</li>
<li>Apply where the role fits your skills — a developer-focused platform means the roles you see are more relevant in the first place.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Use job alerts to be early</h2>
<p>Being among the first qualified applicants matters. Set up <strong>job alerts</strong> for the roles and stacks you want, so you hear about a match the moment it&#39;s posted instead of weeks later when the pipeline is full.</p>
<h2>Prepare for interviews like the job</h2>
<p>Practice the kind of work the role involves, not just abstract puzzles. Be ready to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Walk through a project and the decisions you made.</li>
<li>Talk through a realistic problem out loud.</li>
<li>Ask sharp questions — interviews go both ways, and good questions signal seniority.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Evaluate the company, too</h2>
<p>A job is a two-way fit. Look for:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Transparent salary ranges</strong> — a sign the company is fair.</li>
<li>A <strong>clear, respectful interview process</strong> — a preview of how they operate.</li>
<li>Evidence of a real <strong>engineering culture</strong> — blogs, open source, how the team talks about its work.</li>
</ul>
<p>You want a place that will help you grow, not just any offer.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Standing out isn&#39;t about gaming the system — it&#39;s about showing real evidence of your ability, presenting it clearly, applying with focus, and moving early on the right roles. Do that, and the search shifts from shouting into the void to having good conversations with companies that actually fit.</p>
