3:T1e81,<p>DevOps is the job title most likely to mean something different at every company. To one team it&#39;s a platform engineer building self-service tooling. To another it&#39;s the person who owns Kubernetes. To a third, it&#39;s whoever gets paged at 3am for everything nobody else wants to own. Experienced infrastructure engineers have learned to assume the third one unless a job post proves otherwise.</p>
<p>Your post&#39;s main task, then, is proving otherwise.</p>
<h2>The template</h2>
<h3>DevOps Engineer at [Company]</h3>
<p><strong>Location:</strong> [City / Remote — [region] / Hybrid]
<strong>Compensation:</strong> [$X – $Y] base, plus [equity / bonus / on-call compensation]
<strong>Level:</strong> [Mid-level / Senior / Staff]</p>
<h3>About the role</h3>
<p>[Company] runs [what the platform does] on [cloud provider]. Today that&#39;s [n] services, [n] engineers deploying [n] times a week, with [an honest description of the current state — &quot;a mature platform,&quot; &quot;a working setup with real gaps,&quot; &quot;a lot of manual steps we need to automate&quot;].</p>
<p>We&#39;re hiring a DevOps engineer to own [the specific mandate — build our deployment platform, cut deploy times, get us to reliable multi-region, replace the manual release process].</p>
<p><strong>This is a platform role, not a helpdesk role.</strong> [Say which it truly is. If the job includes internal support, say what share.]</p>
<h3>What you&#39;ll do</h3>
<ul>
<li>Own and evolve our [CI/CD] pipeline so engineers can ship safely without you in the loop.</li>
<li>Manage infrastructure as code with [Terraform / Pulumi / CloudFormation].</li>
<li>Build observability — metrics, logs, tracing, and alerts people actually trust.</li>
<li>Improve reliability: capacity, failure modes, incident response, and the follow-up that stops repeats.</li>
<li>Reduce toil. Anything done manually twice is a candidate for automation.</li>
<li>Partner with product engineers on deployment, scaling, and cost.</li>
</ul>
<h3>What we&#39;re looking for</h3>
<ul>
<li>[n]+ years in DevOps, SRE, platform, or infrastructure engineering.</li>
<li>Strong [AWS / GCP / Azure] experience.</li>
<li>Infrastructure as code in practice, not just in principle.</li>
<li>Container orchestration with [Kubernetes / ECS / Nomad] — including what goes wrong with it.</li>
<li>Comfortable writing real code in [Python / Go / Bash], not only configuration.</li>
<li>Incident experience: you&#39;ve debugged production under pressure and improved things afterward.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Nice to have</h3>
<ul>
<li>[Service mesh / secrets management / policy as code].</li>
<li>Cost optimization at meaningful scale.</li>
<li>Security and compliance work — [SOC 2, HIPAA, PCI] if relevant.</li>
<li>Database operations: backups, replication, and restores you&#39;ve actually tested.</li>
</ul>
<h3>On-call</h3>
<p>We run a [n]-person rotation, [n] week[s] at a time. It currently pages roughly [n] times per month outside working hours. [State compensation or time off in lieu.] Every page gets reviewed, and reducing them is explicitly part of this job.</p>
<h3>Our current setup</h3>
<p>[Cloud, orchestration, IaC tool, CI system, monitoring stack, deployment model.] [One honest line about the worst part — &quot;our staging environment is unreliable and fixing it is your first project.&quot;]</p>
<h3>Interview process</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>[30 min]</strong> Intro call.</li>
<li><strong>[60 min]</strong> Systems and infrastructure conversation, including an incident you&#39;ve handled.</li>
<li><strong>[90 min]</strong> Practical session: debug a realistic failure with us, or design a deployment pipeline together. No trivia quizzes.</li>
<li><strong>[45 min]</strong> Meet the engineers you&#39;d support.</li>
</ol>
<h3>Benefits and how to apply</h3>
<p>[Benefits.] [Application link.] We reply to everyone.</p>
<p>[Company] is an equal opportunity employer.</p>
<h2>Hiring infrastructure engineers: what&#39;s different</h2>
<h3>Name the mandate, or you&#39;ll get nobody good</h3>
<p>&quot;Maintain our infrastructure&quot; attracts nobody. &quot;Cut our deploy time from forty minutes to five&quot; attracts exactly the person who finds that satisfying. Infrastructure engineers are unusually motivated by a concrete mess to fix — give them one to picture.</p>
<h3>On-call terms belong in the post, not the offer call</h3>
<p>For this role in particular, on-call is not a footnote; it&#39;s a core term of employment. Candidates who&#39;ve been burned will assume the worst, and the only way to beat that assumption is specifics: rotation size, frequency, real page volume, and compensation. Publishing honest numbers — even unflattering ones — will win you more candidates than omitting them, because it signals you&#39;re measuring at all.</p>
<h3>Be clear about the support boundary</h3>
<p>The fastest way to lose a platform hire in year one is to hire them for automation and hand them a ticket queue. If part of the role is internal support, say what proportion. Some strong candidates are happy with it; all of them resent discovering it later.</p>
<h3>Screen for judgment over tool inventory</h3>
<p>Tool lists age badly and transfer easily. What distinguishes strong infrastructure candidates is judgment:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>&quot;Tell me about your worst outage.&quot;</strong> You&#39;re listening for honest ownership, a real diagnostic path, and a systemic fix rather than a heroic story.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;What have you deliberately chosen not to automate?&quot;</strong> Great candidates have a considered answer; weaker ones automate reflexively.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;When would you not use Kubernetes?&quot;</strong> Tests whether they reach for complexity by default.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;How do you decide what deserves an alert?&quot;</strong> Alert design reveals how someone thinks about signal, noise, and the humans carrying the pager.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Walk me through a migration you did with no downtime.&quot;</strong> Planning, sequencing, and rollback thinking all surface at once.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Expect to compete on quality of life</h3>
<p>Infrastructure engineers are scarce and are frequently recruited away from teams that page them constantly. The strongest recruiting pitch in this specialty isn&#39;t compensation — it&#39;s a credible claim that the environment is calm, improving, and that reliability work is genuinely resourced. If that&#39;s true where you are, say so with evidence. If it isn&#39;t, the honest version (&quot;it&#39;s noisy right now; fixing it is the job, and here&#39;s the budget&quot;) still recruits well.</p>
<h2>What to delete</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>&quot;DevOps engineer&quot;</strong> for a job that&#39;s really internal IT support — mislabeling wastes everyone&#39;s time.</li>
<li><strong>A twenty-item tool list.</strong> It reads as a wish list assembled from other job posts.</li>
<li><strong>&quot;Fast-paced, high-pressure environment&quot;</strong> in a role that&#39;s already about carrying a pager.</li>
<li><strong>No mention of on-call at all.</strong> Silence is the loudest thing in an infrastructure job post.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Infrastructure candidates are screening you harder than you&#39;re screening them, and they&#39;re screening on one axis above all: will this job wreck my sleep? Name a concrete mandate, publish real on-call numbers, draw the support boundary clearly, admit what&#39;s broken, and interview for judgment rather than tool trivia. Honesty here is not a risk — it&#39;s the differentiator.</p>
