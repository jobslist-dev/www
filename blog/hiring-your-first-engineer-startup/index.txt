3:T1b9b,<p>The first engineering hire is the highest-variance decision a young company makes. Get it right and you gain a partner who compounds — someone who builds the product, sets the technical culture, and later helps you hire everyone else. Get it wrong and you spend a year building the wrong thing on foundations you&#39;ll have to tear out, while burning runway you can&#39;t get back.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s how to approach it, including the case where you can&#39;t personally evaluate the code.</p>
<h2>First, work out which engineer you need</h2>
<p>&quot;We need a developer&quot; isn&#39;t a job. The first hire falls into roughly four categories, and they&#39;re not interchangeable:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The generalist builder.</strong> Ships product across the whole stack, prefers speed over elegance, comfortable with ambiguity. This is what most pre-product-market-fit startups actually need.</li>
<li><strong>The specialist.</strong> Deep in the one thing your product lives or dies on — the ML model, the video pipeline, the trading engine. Right when your core technical risk is genuinely hard.</li>
<li><strong>The technical lead.</strong> Someone senior enough to make architecture decisions and, later, hire and manage. Right when you have funding and intend to grow a team quickly.</li>
<li><strong>The contractor.</strong> Not a hire at all. Right when you need a defined thing built and aren&#39;t sure yet what the ongoing role is.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most first-hire disappointments trace back to hiring one of these while needing another. Write down what has to be true in twelve months, then pick accordingly.</p>
<h2>Be honest about what you&#39;re offering</h2>
<p>You are competing against companies with more money, more stability, and a clearer path. You will lose that comparison on those axes, so don&#39;t compete there. What you have that they don&#39;t:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Real ownership</strong> — of systems, decisions, and direction, immediately.</li>
<li><strong>Visible impact.</strong> Their work is the product, not a feature flag in someone else&#39;s roadmap.</li>
<li><strong>Speed.</strong> No committees, no six-week approval cycles.</li>
<li><strong>Equity that could matter,</strong> with the honest caveat that it usually doesn&#39;t.</li>
<li><strong>Proximity to the business</strong> — engineers who want to understand customers and revenue rarely get to at scale.</li>
</ul>
<p>Lead with those. And be candid about the risks, because the candidates worth hiring will find them anyway: short runway, unclear product direction, and the fact that they&#39;ll be alone technically for a while.</p>
<h2>Pay properly, and don&#39;t over-index on equity</h2>
<p>Underpaying your first engineer is a false economy. You&#39;ll get someone who couldn&#39;t get a better offer, in the role where quality matters most.</p>
<p>A few principles:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Pay as close to market as you can afford,</strong> even if that means hiring later than you&#39;d like.</li>
<li><strong>Don&#39;t offer equity as compensation for below-market salary</strong> unless the candidate genuinely prefers that trade. Most people can&#39;t eat equity.</li>
<li><strong>Be transparent about the equity</strong>: percentage, not just share count. Current valuation, strike price, vesting, and what happens if they leave.</li>
<li><strong>Publish a range in the job post.</strong> For a company nobody has heard of, refusing to name a number is a significant deterrent.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Where to actually find them</h2>
<p>Cold job posts work less well when your brand is unknown. The first hire usually comes from a narrower channel:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Your extended network.</strong> Not close friends — the second ring. Ask specific people for specific introductions.</li>
<li><strong>Developer-focused job boards,</strong> where the audience is technical and your post isn&#39;t buried under thousands of generic listings.</li>
<li><strong>Communities around your stack or domain</strong> — where people who care about your specific problem already gather.</li>
<li><strong>People who have already engaged with your product.</strong> A user who emails you a bug report with a proposed fix is a warm lead.</li>
</ul>
<p>Whatever the channel, reach out personally. A founder-written message about a specific problem out-converts recruiter outreach dramatically.</p>
<h2>Assessing without a technical co-founder</h2>
<p>This is the part non-technical founders dread, and it&#39;s more manageable than it looks.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bring in a trusted engineer for one round.</strong> An advisor, an investor&#39;s technical partner, or a paid consultant for two hours. This is the single highest-ROI thing you can do, and it&#39;s cheap relative to the mistake it prevents.</li>
<li><strong>Do a paid trial project.</strong> One to three days of real work, paid properly. It&#39;s the strongest signal available, for both sides, and far more informative than any interview.</li>
<li><strong>Judge what you <em>can</em> judge.</strong> Do they ask good questions about the business? Can they explain a technical trade-off in terms you understand? Do they push back when you&#39;re wrong? Are they curious about customers? These predict a great deal, and you&#39;re fully qualified to assess them.</li>
<li><strong>Ask them to walk you through past work</strong> and listen for whether the explanation makes sense to you. An engineer who can&#39;t make their work legible to a non-technical founder will be hard to work with for years.</li>
<li><strong>Check references properly,</strong> and ask one question: &quot;Would you work with them again, and why?&quot;</li>
</ul>
<p>Be wary of anyone who makes you feel stupid for asking questions. That trait doesn&#39;t improve after they join.</p>
<h2>Set them up to succeed</h2>
<p>The hire isn&#39;t done when they sign.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Give them ownership of something whole</strong>, not fragments of your ideas.</li>
<li><strong>Write down what you&#39;re trying to learn,</strong> not just what you want built. Engineers who understand the goal make better decisions when the spec is silent.</li>
<li><strong>Agree how technical decisions get made</strong> — especially the ones you&#39;re not equipped to arbitrate.</li>
<li><strong>Expect them to disagree with you.</strong> You hired judgment; ignoring it wastes the money.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Decide which of the four roles you actually need, pay close to market rather than compensating with optimism, recruit through personal channels, and borrow technical judgment for at least one round of assessment. A paid trial project will tell you more than any interview loop. And once they&#39;re in, give them a real problem and the authority to solve it — that&#39;s the entire reason a strong engineer would join you instead of somewhere safer.</p>
