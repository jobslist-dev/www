3:Te0f,<p>When teams budget for hiring, they usually think about the obvious line item — the cost of posting a job. But that&#39;s a tiny fraction of what a hire actually costs. Understanding the full picture helps you spend where it matters and avoid the most expensive mistake of all: a bad hire.</p>
<h2>The visible costs</h2>
<p>These are the ones that show up on an invoice:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Job postings</strong> — the fee to advertise the role.</li>
<li><strong>Tools and subscriptions</strong> — applicant tracking, assessment platforms.</li>
<li><strong>Recruiter or agency fees</strong> — often a large percentage of first-year salary, if you go that route.</li>
</ul>
<p>These are real, but they&#39;re rarely the biggest number.</p>
<h2>The hidden costs</h2>
<p>This is where the real money is:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Your team&#39;s time.</strong> Every hour spent screening unqualified résumés, running interviews, and debriefing has a cost. Multiply engineer and manager hours by their effective rate and the number gets large fast.</li>
<li><strong>Time-to-hire.</strong> While a role sits open, the work doesn&#39;t get done — or it lands on an already-stretched team, risking burnout and attrition.</li>
<li><strong>Onboarding and ramp-up.</strong> A new hire isn&#39;t fully productive on day one. Weeks or months of ramp are part of the cost.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The most expensive cost: a bad hire</h2>
<p>A mis-hire is dramatically more expensive than any posting fee. You pay for the original search, the salary during a tenure that doesn&#39;t work out, the lost productivity and team morale, and then the cost of doing the whole search again. This is why investing in <em>quality</em> up front — better-fit candidates and a sharper process — pays for itself many times over.</p>
<h2>How to spend smarter</h2>
<p>Given that screening time and bad hires dominate the true cost, the smartest moves reduce <em>those</em>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Post where fit is high.</strong> A developer-focused board yields better-matched candidates, cutting screening time even if the headline fee is similar.</li>
<li><strong>Demand pricing transparency.</strong> Opaque, auction-style pricing makes budgeting impossible. Look for clear, predictable costs.</li>
<li><strong>Match the model to your pattern.</strong> Most companies hire in bursts, not continuously. Non-expiring listing packs let you pay only when you&#39;re actually hiring, instead of carrying a subscription through quiet months — and a good platform will show you which option is cheaper for your volume.</li>
<li><strong>Tighten your process.</strong> A faster, more accurate loop reduces both team-time and time-to-hire.</li>
</ul>
<h2>A quick mental model</h2>
<p>Think of hiring cost as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(Posting + tools) + (team hours × rate) + (days open × cost of the gap) + (risk of mis-hire × its cost)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When you frame it this way, it&#39;s obvious that shaving a few dollars off a posting fee is the wrong place to optimize. Reducing screening time and mis-hire risk is where the leverage is.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>The job-post fee is the smallest part of what hiring costs. The real money is in your team&#39;s time, the gap while a role sits open, and the risk of getting it wrong. Spend to improve <em>fit</em> and <em>speed</em> — transparent, flexible pricing and a developer-focused audience do exactly that.</p>
