3:Td38,<p>You&#39;ve got the offer — congratulations. Now don&#39;t leave money on the table. Negotiating feels uncomfortable, but it&#39;s expected, low-risk when done well, and one conversation can be worth thousands of dollars a year compounded over your career. Here&#39;s how to do it without jeopardizing the offer.</p>
<h2>First: companies expect you to negotiate</h2>
<p>A respectful negotiation almost never loses an offer — employers build in room and a polite ask signals confidence. The risk isn&#39;t asking; it&#39;s asking badly (ultimatums, inflated bluffs, or going silent). Approach it as a collaborative conversation, not a fight.</p>
<h2>Know your number before you talk</h2>
<p>Walk in informed:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Research the market</strong> for the role, level, and location (and remote norms). Use salary data, peers, and any transparent ranges.</li>
<li>Define three numbers: your <strong>walk-away minimum</strong>, a <strong>realistic target</strong>, and an <strong>ambitious-but-defensible</strong> ask to open with.</li>
<li>Factor in <strong>total compensation</strong>, not just base — equity, bonus, benefits, PTO.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Let them name a number first when you can</h2>
<p>If asked your expectations early, it&#39;s fine to deflect politely: &quot;I&#39;d like to learn more about the role and scope first — what range is budgeted for this position?&quot; If pressed, give a researched range with your target near the bottom of it. Anchoring on solid data protects you from lowballing yourself.</p>
<h2>Make the ask cleanly</h2>
<p>When the offer arrives:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Express genuine enthusiasm first</strong> — &quot;I&#39;m excited about this; I&#39;d love to make it work.&quot;</li>
<li><strong>Anchor with a specific, justified number</strong>: &quot;Based on my experience with X and market data for this level, I was targeting $Y.&quot;</li>
<li>Then <strong>stop talking</strong> and let them respond. Silence is fine.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Negotiate the whole package</h2>
<p>If base is capped, there&#39;s often flexibility elsewhere:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Signing bonus</strong> to bridge a gap.</li>
<li><strong>Equity</strong> (understand vesting and what it&#39;s really worth).</li>
<li><strong>Start date, PTO, remote flexibility, learning budget, title.</strong></li>
<li>A <strong>6-month review</strong> with a defined raise path.</li>
</ul>
<p>Asking &quot;is there flexibility on…&quot; is collaborative and frequently works.</p>
<h2>Get it in writing, and be gracious</h2>
<ul>
<li>Once you agree, ask for the <strong>updated offer in writing</strong> before resigning anywhere.</li>
<li>Whatever the outcome, stay <strong>warm and professional</strong> — you&#39;ll be working with these people.</li>
<li>Don&#39;t bluff with fake competing offers; if you have a real one, you can mention it honestly.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Negotiating is expected and rarely costs you the offer when you&#39;re enthusiastic, informed, and specific. Know your numbers, let them anchor when possible, ask cleanly for a justified figure, and negotiate the whole package — then get it in writing. One short conversation can pay off for years.</p>
