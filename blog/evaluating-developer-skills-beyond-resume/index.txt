3:Tdc0,<p>A résumé tells you where someone worked and what they claim to know. It tells you very little about whether they&#39;ll write good code, work well with your team, or solve the problems you actually have. If you over-index on résumés, you&#39;ll filter out brilliant self-taught engineers and over-rate people who simply had impressive logos.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s how to evaluate what really matters.</p>
<h2>What résumés get wrong</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Pedigree bias.</strong> A famous employer or degree correlates weakly with day-to-day performance.</li>
<li><strong>Keyword theater.</strong> Listing a technology isn&#39;t the same as being good at it.</li>
<li><strong>Gaps and nonlinear paths.</strong> Career breaks, bootcamps, and self-teaching often hide your strongest, most motivated candidates.</li>
</ul>
<p>Treat the résumé as a conversation starter, not a verdict.</p>
<h2>Look at real work</h2>
<p>The best signal is evidence of actual building:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Open-source contributions</strong> or a <strong>portfolio</strong> — even small projects reveal a lot about how someone codes and thinks.</li>
<li><strong>A GitHub profile</strong> with real commits, not just forks.</li>
<li><strong>Side projects</strong> — they show curiosity and initiative, two traits that predict growth.</li>
</ul>
<p>You&#39;re not looking for volume; you&#39;re looking for thoughtfulness.</p>
<h2>Use realistic, job-like exercises</h2>
<p>The closer your assessment is to the real job, the better it predicts performance. A small, paid take-home or a pairing session on a realistic problem beats abstract puzzles every time. Watch not just whether they get the right answer, but <em>how</em> they get there — how they break down the problem, handle ambiguity, and explain their thinking.</p>
<h2>Assess the traits that compound</h2>
<p>Specific framework knowledge fades; underlying traits compound. Probe for:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Problem decomposition</strong> — can they take something fuzzy and make it concrete?</li>
<li><strong>Communication</strong> — can they explain a technical decision to a non-expert?</li>
<li><strong>Curiosity and learning speed</strong> — the stack will change; the ability to learn won&#39;t.</li>
<li><strong>Collaboration</strong> — how they react to feedback and to a better idea than their own.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Structure your evaluation</h2>
<p>To keep things fair and comparable:</p>
<ol>
<li>Define the <strong>handful of competencies</strong> the role truly needs.</li>
<li>Map <strong>each interview stage</strong> to one or two of them.</li>
<li>Score against a <strong>rubric</strong>, not a gut feeling.</li>
<li>Have interviewers write notes <strong>before</strong> discussing, to avoid groupthink.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Beware your own biases</h2>
<p>Unstructured interviews amplify bias — toward people who look, talk, or think like the interviewer. Structure, rubrics, and work-sample tests are the best-known defenses. They also happen to make your hiring more accurate, so it&#39;s a win-win.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Hire for demonstrated ability and compounding traits, not for résumé keywords and logos. Look at real work, use job-like exercises, and evaluate against a clear rubric. You&#39;ll surface talented people that résumé-screening would have missed — and that&#39;s a real edge.</p>
