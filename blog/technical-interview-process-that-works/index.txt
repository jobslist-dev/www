3:Tef0,<p>The point of a technical interview is simple: predict whether someone will do great work on your team. Yet many processes test things that have little to do with the actual job — obscure algorithms, trivia, or the ability to perform under artificial pressure. The result is that companies reject people who&#39;d excel and hire people who interview well but struggle in the role.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s how to design a loop that&#39;s accurate, fair, and fast.</p>
<h2>Test the work, not a proxy for the work</h2>
<p>The strongest predictor of job performance is a sample of the actual job. If the role is building APIs, have them build a small API. If it&#39;s debugging gnarly production issues, give them a realistic bug to find. Abstract puzzles only make sense if the job is genuinely about those puzzles.</p>
<p>Good formats:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>A short, paid take-home</strong> that mirrors real work and respects their time (aim for 2–3 hours, not a weekend).</li>
<li><strong>A pairing session</strong> where you solve a realistic problem together — this also reveals how they collaborate.</li>
<li><strong>A code review exercise</strong> where they critique a small PR, which shows judgment and communication.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Standardize so you can compare</h2>
<p>Ad-hoc interviews produce ad-hoc results. Define:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <strong>questions or exercises</strong> each candidate gets.</li>
<li>A <strong>rubric</strong> for what &quot;strong,&quot; &quot;okay,&quot; and &quot;weak&quot; look like.</li>
<li>Who <strong>owns each stage</strong> and what they&#39;re assessing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Structure reduces bias and lets you compare candidates on the same axes instead of vibes.</p>
<h2>Respect the candidate&#39;s time — and yours</h2>
<p>Great candidates are evaluating you, too. A bloated, slow process signals a bloated, slow company. Keep the loop tight:</p>
<ol>
<li>Recruiter or hiring-manager screen (30 min).</li>
<li>One technical exercise (take-home or pairing).</li>
<li>One team/values conversation.</li>
<li>Decision within days, not weeks.</li>
</ol>
<p>Every extra round and every extra day increases your chance of losing the person to a faster competitor.</p>
<h2>Assess collaboration and communication</h2>
<p>Software is a team sport. Beyond raw ability, look for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Can they explain their reasoning clearly?</li>
<li>Do they ask good questions when requirements are ambiguous?</li>
<li>How do they respond to feedback or a better idea?</li>
</ul>
<p>These traits often matter more than knowing one more framework.</p>
<h2>Avoid the common traps</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Trick questions</strong> — they test nerves, not skill.</li>
<li><strong>Unpaid, multi-day take-homes</strong> — they screen out people with families and current jobs (often your best candidates).</li>
<li><strong>Interviewer inconsistency</strong> — five interviewers asking five random things isn&#39;t a signal, it&#39;s noise.</li>
<li><strong>Culture &quot;fit&quot; as a vibe check</strong> — define the values you&#39;re actually assessing, or you&#39;ll just hire people like yourself.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Close the loop with feedback</h2>
<p>Whether or not you make an offer, prompt and respectful communication protects your reputation. Developers talk, and a candidate who had a great experience — even after a rejection — will refer others and may reapply later.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A technical interview should look like the job, run on a clear rubric, and move fast. Test real work, standardize your evaluation, and treat candidates like the professionals they are. You&#39;ll make better decisions and win more of the people you want.</p>
