3:Tf89,<p>Most job descriptions are written for the company, not the candidate. They list every technology the team has ever touched, demand &quot;5+ years&quot; of a framework that&#39;s three years old, and bury the interesting parts under boilerplate. Then companies wonder why they only get unqualified applicants.</p>
<p>The job description is the single highest-leverage piece of your hiring funnel. Here&#39;s how to write one that pulls in the right people.</p>
<h2>Lead with the problem, not the checklist</h2>
<p>Developers are drawn to interesting problems. Open with what they&#39;ll actually work on and why it matters:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>&quot;You&#39;ll own the systems that process millions of payments a month, where a 100ms improvement is real money and reliability is non-negotiable.&quot;</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That one sentence does more than a bullet list of &quot;Node.js, PostgreSQL, AWS&quot; ever could. Save the tech stack for later — it&#39;s a filter, not a hook.</p>
<h2>Be honest about the level and scope</h2>
<p>Calibrate the title and expectations to reality. If you need someone who can operate independently and mentor others, say so. If it&#39;s a role where someone will grow, say that too. Misleading seniority labels waste everyone&#39;s time and erode trust the moment the interview starts.</p>
<h2>State the salary range</h2>
<p>This is the highest-impact change most companies can make. Listings with transparent compensation:</p>
<ul>
<li>Get <strong>more</strong> applications, and</li>
<li>Get <strong>better-matched</strong> applications, because people self-select.</li>
</ul>
<p>Hiding the range signals that you might lowball, and the best candidates simply skip listings without it. Transparency is a competitive advantage.</p>
<h2>Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves</h2>
<p>A wall of &quot;requirements&quot; scares off exactly the candidates you want — strong developers who don&#39;t tick every box will pass, while overconfident ones apply anyway. Keep must-haves to the genuine essentials (usually three to five), and clearly label everything else as a bonus.</p>
<h2>Cut the clichés</h2>
<p>Phrases to delete on sight:</p>
<ul>
<li>&quot;Rockstar,&quot; &quot;ninja,&quot; &quot;guru,&quot; &quot;wizard&quot;</li>
<li>&quot;Work hard, play hard&quot;</li>
<li>&quot;We&#39;re like a family&quot;</li>
<li>&quot;Must thrive in a fast-paced environment&quot; (often code for chaos)</li>
</ul>
<p>Replace them with specifics. Instead of &quot;fast-paced,&quot; describe your actual cadence: &quot;We ship to production multiple times a day.&quot;</p>
<h2>Make the logistics obvious</h2>
<p>Answer the questions every candidate has before they apply:</p>
<ul>
<li>Is it remote, hybrid, or onsite? If hybrid, how many days?</li>
<li>What&#39;s the interview process and how long does it take?</li>
<li>What does the team look like and who will they work with?</li>
</ul>
<p>Removing uncertainty removes friction, and friction is where good applicants drop off.</p>
<h2>A simple structure that works</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>One-line hook</strong> — the mission and the problem.</li>
<li><strong>What you&#39;ll do</strong> — 4–6 concrete responsibilities.</li>
<li><strong>What we&#39;re looking for</strong> — must-haves, short and honest.</li>
<li><strong>Nice to have</strong> — clearly optional.</li>
<li><strong>Compensation &amp; logistics</strong> — range, location, process.</li>
<li><strong>About us</strong> — brief, human, specific.</li>
</ol>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>A great job description is short, honest, specific, and written for the person reading it. It respects their time, answers their questions, and leads with something worth being excited about. Do that and your application quality will climb before you change anything else.</p>
