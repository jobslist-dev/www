3:Te42,<p>The hardest part of hiring isn&#39;t always finding the right person — it&#39;s closing them. Strong developers often have multiple offers, and a great candidate lost at the offer stage means starting the whole search over. Here&#39;s how to build an offer that wins and present it so your top choice accepts.</p>
<h2>The close starts at the first interaction</h2>
<p>By the time you extend an offer, the candidate has already formed an opinion of you. A fast, respectful, well-communicated process is itself part of the offer. If the experience was disorganized or slow, no amount of money fully fixes it. Treat every stage as part of closing.</p>
<h2>Get the compensation right</h2>
<p>Money isn&#39;t everything, but getting it wrong ends the conversation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Know the market.</strong> Underpaying relative to the role and location guarantees you lose people — and wastes everyone&#39;s time.</li>
<li><strong>Be transparent early.</strong> Sharing the range up front avoids the painful surprise where you fall far below expectations after weeks of interviews.</li>
<li><strong>Think total comp.</strong> Base, any bonus or equity, and benefits all matter. Be clear about each so the candidate can compare honestly.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Sell more than salary</h2>
<p>Top developers weigh more than the number:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The work.</strong> Interesting problems and real impact are powerful draws.</li>
<li><strong>Growth.</strong> Will they learn and advance? Who will they learn from?</li>
<li><strong>The team and culture.</strong> People join teams they respect and managers they trust.</li>
<li><strong>Flexibility.</strong> Remote options and autonomy carry real weight.</li>
</ul>
<p>Make the case for why <em>this</em> role is a great move for <em>them</em>, specifically.</p>
<h2>Present the offer like you mean it</h2>
<p>Don&#39;t email a PDF and wait. Make it personal:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Call them.</strong> Express genuine enthusiasm — people want to feel wanted.</li>
<li><strong>Walk through the details</strong> so nothing is ambiguous.</li>
<li><strong>Explain the &quot;why&quot;</strong> — why you&#39;re excited to have them and where they&#39;ll make an impact.</li>
</ul>
<p>Enthusiasm is contagious and often the deciding factor between two similar offers.</p>
<h2>Handle negotiation gracefully</h2>
<p>Expect some negotiation and treat it as normal, not adversarial:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Listen to what actually matters</strong> to them — it may be flexibility or scope, not just base pay.</li>
<li><strong>Move quickly.</strong> Drawn-out back-and-forth cools excitement.</li>
<li><strong>Know your limits</strong> in advance so you can respond without delay.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Set a reasonable deadline</h2>
<p>Give them enough time to decide thoughtfully, but not so long that momentum dies. A clear, fair timeline keeps the process moving and signals confidence.</p>
<h2>After they accept</h2>
<p>Closing doesn&#39;t end at &quot;yes.&quot; Stay in touch between acceptance and start date, get onboarding ready, and keep them excited. Candidates can still be poached after accepting — a warm, organized pre-start experience protects your win.</p>
<h2>The takeaway</h2>
<p>Winning your top candidate is about competitive, transparent compensation <em>plus</em> a compelling story and a personal, enthusiastic close. Respect their time, sell the whole opportunity, and move fast. Do that and you&#39;ll convert far more of the people you most want to hire.</p>
